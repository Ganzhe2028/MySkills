# Research Foundation For 刨根问底

This skill turns the user's self-study pattern into an evidence-aligned learning routine. The point is not to cite research in every user answer; the point is to let research shape the workflow.

## Core translation

| Learning-science idea | What it means for this skill |
| --- | --- |
| Prior knowledge shapes new learning | Start by checking what the learner already knows. Missing or wrong foundations must be found before the target concept can be stable. |
| Cognitive load | Do not overload working memory. Repair the lowest necessary layer, then rebuild upward in small steps. |
| Retrieval practice | Ask the learner to recall, solve, classify, or explain from memory. Rereading is not enough. |
| Spacing and desirable difficulty | Make practice effortful and revisit it later. Short-term struggle can support long-term retention when the task is still reachable. |
| Self-explanation and generative learning | Ask the learner to explain why each step follows. Explanation forces selection, organization, and integration. |
| ICAP / active learning | Push the learner from passive reading toward constructive and interactive work: explain, predict, compare, teach back. |
| Self-regulated learning | Help the learner plan, monitor, diagnose, and adapt their own study process. |
| Transfer | End with near-transfer and far-transfer checks so the learner can use the idea outside the original example. |

## Why “down then up” is defensible

The user's method has two halves:

- **Downward diagnosis**: keep asking what the current idea depends on until the missing root is found.
- **Upward reconstruction**: rebuild the idea from that root and check whether the learner can use it.

This maps well to the research consensus that effective learning depends on prior knowledge, active construction, metacognitive monitoring, retrieval, and transfer. It also avoids the common failure of “deep explanation” skills: explaining more and more without testing whether the learner can actually use the explanation.

## Design implications

### 1. Diagnose before teaching

Use a small probe before giving a full explanation. A few targeted questions usually reveal whether the blocker is vocabulary, notation, prerequisite facts, procedure, concept distinction, or transfer.

### 2. Keep the prerequisite ladder small

The skill should not recursively expand into a whole encyclopedia. Descend only until the learner can support the next inference. A useful foundation is:

- necessary for the target
- currently weak or missing
- teachable in the current session
- testable with a short retrieval or application prompt

### 3. Convert explanation into action

Every repaired layer should include:

- one example
- one formal statement or rule
- one common trap
- one retrieval question
- one self-explanation prompt

### 4. Rebuild explicitly

After repairing a root layer, walk back upward. The learner should see why each step is allowed, not just what the next step is.

### 5. Close with transfer

Understanding is not proven by agreeing with the explanation. Close with one similar case and one surface-different case. If the learner fails, return to the lowest broken layer.

## Evidence base

- National Academies of Sciences, Engineering, and Medicine, *How People Learn II* (2018): emphasizes that effective instruction depends on prior knowledge, experiences, motivation, metacognition, and assessment that narrows the gap between current and desired performance. <https://www.nationalacademies.org/read/24783/chapter/9>
- Dunlosky et al. (2013), *Improving Students' Learning With Effective Learning Techniques*: identifies practice testing and distributed practice as high-utility strategies across many learners and contexts, while rereading and underlining are lower utility. <https://www.psychologicalscience.org/publications/journals/pspi/learning-techniques.html/comment-page-1>
- Yang et al. (2021), *Retrieval Practice Consistently Benefits Student Learning*: classroom-focused systematic review screening nearly 2000 abstracts and coding 50 experiments; retrieval practice showed broad benefits across education levels and content areas. <https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10648-021-09595-9>
- Hopkins et al. (2022), *Spaced Retrieval Practice Imposes Desirable Difficulty in Calculus Learning*: in calculus, spaced retrieval lowered short-term quiz performance but improved later retention, illustrating the learning-performance distinction. <https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10648-022-09677-2>
- Sweller, van Merrienboer, and Paas (2019), *Cognitive Architecture and Instructional Design: 20 Years Later*: cognitive load theory treats working memory as limited and long-term memory as the source of usable schemas; instruction should account for that architecture. <https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10648-019-09465-5>
- Chi and Wylie (2014), *The ICAP Framework*: predicts learning gains as engagement moves from passive to active, constructive, and interactive modes. <https://education.asu.edu/sites/default/files/lcl/chiwylie2014icap_2.pdf>
- Zhang and Fiorella (2024), *Effects of self-explaining feedback on learning from problem-solving errors*: scaffolded self-explanation helped learners correct errors and improve near transfer in physics problem solving. <https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0361476X24000717>
- Zheng and Sun (2024/2025), *Self-Regulated Learning and Learning Outcomes in Undergraduate and Graduate Medical Education*: meta-analysis finding a positive association between self-regulated learning strategies and learning outcomes. <https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/01632787241288849>

## Practical cautions

- “刨根问底” should not mean infinite regress. Stop at the lowest layer that restores the target reasoning.
- Harder is not automatically better. Difficulty is useful only when the learner can still make progress.
- A beautiful explanation is not enough. The learner must retrieve, explain, or transfer.
- Study plans should be narrow enough to execute. The first working loop matters more than a complete curriculum map.
